Controls

Status: partial

Overview

The throw is a three-step, stop-the-meter sequence modeled on SNES Super Bowling: line up the shot, then stop a sweeping meter to set spin/angle, then stop a second sweeping meter to set power. Each step is a single confirm input (tap, click, or button press), so the scheme is precise, learnable, and identical across phone and desktop. It is also a perfect fit for async multiplayer (section 05): no swipe finesse, no drag, just three taps anyone can make on a phone in ten seconds.

This borrows only the control flow from SNES Super Bowling. Everything else, the string pinsetter, the mechanical aesthetic, the duckpin rules, is VibePins. The meter visuals are re-skinned as mechanical gauges (see feedback), not pixel-art arrows.

Step 1: Line up the shot

The player sets the starting position and aim of the ball across the lane. A lateral indicator (the arrow-bar at the top of the SNES reference, with the ball sliding left and right between an L and an R marker) lets the player choose where on the foul line the ball starts and the base direction it points. Move left/right, then confirm to lock the line. This is the deliberate, untimed step; the next two are timed.

Step 2: Stop the spin/angle meter

A meter sweeps back and forth (the oscillating colored bar in the reference). Where the player stops it sets the ball's spin and hook: stopping near center yields a straight, low-spin ball; stopping toward a side imparts left or right spin that curves the ball as it travels. The sweeping cursor moves at a steady, readable speed. One confirm input stops it. The chosen value is the lateral/spin component of the launch.

Step 3: Stop the power meter

A second meter sweeps (the POWER meter in the reference). Where the player stops it sets ball speed. The meter reads as a gradient where a sweet spot is best and the extremes are weak or wild, so the timing carries real risk and reward. One confirm input stops it and triggers the release.

Release: combining the three inputs

On the power stop, the three inputs resolve into the physics launch: line-up sets the starting position and base aim direction, the spin value sets angular velocity (and the resulting curve), and the power value sets initial speed. These feed the ball body in Rapier (see reuse and tech). Spin curves the ball during the roll via a lateral force proportional to spin while the ball is in contact with the lane, decaying as it slows. After release the shot is locked; there is no mid-roll steering in v1.

Input mapping

  • Touch: drag or tap the side zones to move the line-up; tap anywhere to confirm and to stop each meter.
  • Mouse: move to set the line-up; click to confirm and to stop each meter.
  • Keyboard: left/right arrows to set the line-up; space/enter to confirm and to stop each meter.
  • Every step uses the same single confirm action, so the whole throw is "set line, tap, tap".

Feedback and skin

The two sweeping meters are styled as mechanical gauges consistent with look and feel: needle gauges or sliding indicators on riveted metal plates rather than the SNES pixel bars, with a satisfying mechanical click on each stop. The line-up indicator reads as a track the ball slides along. Sweep speed and sweet-spot width are tunable constants (Q-010 resolved: start forgiving, tighten via playtest) and live in the central config (see LANE config).

HUD: reduced text and the throw light

The in-game HUD leans on two persistent surfaces, not prose. The split-flap scoreboard is the source of truth for frame, ball, and score, and a single round red/green go/stop light is the at-a-glance "is it my turn to throw" cue. The light is a physical 3D fixture, not an on-screen overlay: a Pins Mechanical style traffic signal mounted down-lane above the pin deck, facing the bowler, with a red lens stacked over a green lens, visible the length of the lane (built in src/world3d.ts from the THROW_LIGHT_3D config). GREEN means the rack is set and the bowler is ready to aim, set spin and power, and throw. RED means the machine owns the lane: the ball is rolling, the rack is settling, the pinsetter is setting pins, the ball is loading and the bowler is walking up, or the game is over. The same RED state also reads "not your turn" in an async-match line, so the rule is one consistent thing across solo and match.

Timing matters: the aiming phase begins during the ball load and walk-up, while the pinsetter may still be setting the rack, so the signal stays RED through the whole reset / pin-setting cycle and the walk-up and turns GREEN only once the rack is set and the bowler has reached the line (the camera is at align / locked). The signal is not colour-only: a visually-hidden accessible mirror (#throw-light in index.html, role=status with an aria-label that reads "Ready to throw" / "Wait") announces the state to screen readers so it never depends on the 3D colour alone. The verbose status sentence is gone: no "Frame N", "Foul!", "Gutter ball.", "Counting pins...", "Rolling...", or "Game over" line. The first-run tutorial coach still names the three throw steps, and a single transient status line remains for the async-match frame submit. The final score lives on the summary overlay, not the live HUD. The mapping is pure (throwLightFor(phase, setup) with readyToAim in src/shotLoop.ts) and unit-tested: green only when the rack is set and the bowler is at the line, red through setting / loading / ball-in-motion / over.
