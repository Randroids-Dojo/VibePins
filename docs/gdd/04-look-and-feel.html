Look and feel

Status: partial

Reference: Pins Mechanical Co., taken literally

The visual and tonal reference is Pins Mechanical Co., and VibePins takes the "Mechanical" literally. Where the venue brand reads as a retro bar, VibePins pushes the machinery to the foreground: this is a world of exposed mechanisms, cords and pulleys, motors and relays, brass and steel and oiled wood. The mood is industrial and tactile, a little retro-futurist, with the warmth of a well-used machine room. Not neon-arcade, not slick-corporate. The fun is watching honest mechanism do its work.

The Rube Goldberg principle

The defining aesthetic move: the string pinsetter (see string pinsetter) is staged as a Rube Goldberg contraption you get to watch. Cords, guide tubes, pulleys, winding drums, and the overhead drive unit are all visible and legible. Nothing important is hidden behind a panel. The reset is a small mechanical performance: cords go taut, drums turn, pins rise on their strings, the rack swings into place, everything clicks home. Half the satisfaction of the game is watching the machine work, so the machine is on stage, not in a closet.

The venue and lane

A single, focused 3D scene: one duckpin lane inside a machine-room interior. The lane is shorter than a regulation alley, consistent with the casual venue. Above and behind the pins sits the visible pinsetter rig: a frame of beams, ten guide tubes, ten cords running up to winding drums and the overhead drive unit. Background suggests the room through more machinery (gears, conduit, gauges, the silhouette of other lane rigs, warm work-lights) rather than neon signage or an explorable space. Camera sits behind the foul line looking down the lane, framed so the overhead string rig is clearly in view.

Palette, materials, and lighting

  • Material-led palette: oiled hardwood lane, brushed and blackened steel, aged brass, copper, cast iron, leather and canvas. Warm metals over cool plastics.
  • Accent color comes from the machine, not signage: amber indicator lamps, the deep red of a painted frame, the glint of polished brass, hazard-stripe yellow on moving parts.
  • Lighting is warm industrial work-light: focused pools over the lane and the pinsetter rig, soft falloff into a dim machine room. Scene fog for depth and grime in the air.
  • Surfaces show use: scuffs, oil sheen, worn edges, dust. Honest wear, not clean CGI.
  • Glossy lane reflecting warm highlights; pins (real duckpins are painted wood, often with red neck bands) lit by the work-lights.

UI and scoreboard

The scoreboard reads as a piece of machinery, not a glowing screen: a split-flap or rolling-counter mechanical display, or an electromechanical scoreboard with relay-driven digits and small indicator bulbs. Frame marks (strike, spare, flat ten, open) flip or roll into place with a click. Menus and HUD use sturdy industrial type (stencil, embossed plate, engraved gauge faces) on metal-plate panels with rivets and toggle switches. Keep on-screen text minimal; the game should read without instructions (pillar 4).

Audio

Audio uses a procedural Web Audio engine in the same style as Hoops (an AudioEngine class that synthesizes sounds rather than shipping audio files: lazy init on first gesture, resume-on-suspend, a global enable toggle). The palette is mechanical, not musical-arcade. See reuse and tech.

  • Pin clatter: the dull, woody knock of squat duckpins, not the bright crack of tenpins. Lower energy, less scatter.
  • Ball roll: a short rumble down the wood, a release thunk on launch.
  • String reset: the signature sound bed. Servo whir, winding drums, taut-cord twang, clicking relays, the rack thunking home. This is the machine's voice.
  • Ambient: a low machine-room hum, distant clanks and motors, the occasional hiss or relay click, mixed quiet under the action.
  • Stings: a satisfying mechanical flourish on the rare strike (bells, a ratchet run, a steam hiss); smaller mechanical cues for spares and good rolls.

Juice and feedback

Pillar 4 demands immediate, satisfying feedback, expressed mechanically. On a strike: the contraption runs a brief Rube Goldberg victory routine (a chain reaction through the visible mechanism, bells, sparks or steam, indicator lamps lighting in sequence) plus a screen shake and a sound flourish, sized to how rare strikes are in duckpin. On a spare: a smaller mechanical flourish. Every ball gives clear, fast confirmation of the count. The string lift itself is core juice: it is genuinely fun to watch the pins get reeled up. Avoid long, unskippable animations; keep the loop fast.

Build log

  • Visible pinsetter rig landed (REQ-040 done). The string machine is now staged above the pin deck as set dressing. pinsetterRigParts in src/config.ts is pure layout derived from the rack home spots and a single tunable PINSETTER block: two longitudinal painted-red rails plus a cross beam over each pin row at the cord anchor height (PINSETTER.frameTopY = TETHER.topY, so the beams visibly carry the strings the pins already hang from), one blackened-steel guide tube hung below the frame over each pin (above the standing pins, below the frame), one winding drum per pin riding a per-row cross-shaft, and a dark cast-iron overhead drive unit mounted at the down-lane back end. world3d.buildPinsetterRig turns the parts into box and cylinder meshes (rigBeamMesh / rigCylinderMesh); the cords themselves were already rendered per pin in src/pins.ts (REQ-013). No colliders: the rig is dressing and never touches physics or the cords' behaviour. The pure layout is unit tested headless (one tube and drum per pin, one shaft and cross beam per row, frame at anchor height, tubes clear of the standing pins, drive unit above and behind the rack, frame within lane width). Files: src/config.ts, src/world3d.ts, tests/pinsetter.test.ts. PR #TBD.
  • Electromechanical scoreboard skin landed (REQ-042 done). The plain readable board is now a split-flap display bolted to a blackened-steel plate. In src/scoreboard.ts every ball glyph and running total renders on a two-leaf split-flap card (a top and bottom leaf clipped to their halves, split by a centre hinge seam) carrying a stable data-cell key. The Scoreboard class keeps the glyphs from the last paint and, on the next paint, computes which cells changed via two pure helpers (cellGlyphs flattens a score to one cell per ball/total slot, changedCellKeys diffs two boards), then adds .vp-flip to just those cards so their top leaf folds over the hinge and back via the vp-flap-fold keyframe in index.html, the way a real split-flap board snaps a new digit, and clears the class after the animation window. The flip is suppressed for users who ask to reduce motion (a media query plus a matchMedia guard in JS so the board still updates, it just snaps). The pure diff is unit tested headless and the class is verified against a fake DOM (RULE 10 observable motion). Files: src/scoreboard.ts, index.html, tests/scoreboard.test.ts, tests/scoreboard.flip.test.ts. PR #TBD.
  • Procedural audio engine landed (REQ-043 done). New src/audio.ts with an AudioEngine class ported in spirit from Hoops: it synthesizes every sound from oscillators and filtered noise (no audio files), lazily creates the AudioContext on the first user gesture, resumes a suspended context, and honours a global enable flag wired to the persisted audio setting. Mechanical palette: a dull woody duckpin clatter that scales loudness with pins downed, a ball roll rumble and release thunk on launch, the signature string-reset bed (servo whir, taut-cord twang, relay clicks, the rack thunking home), a brassy strike sting with a steam-hiss tail, a smaller spare flourish, and a relay-style UI click. The AudioContext is created through an injectable factory so the engine is unit-tested headless. src/main.ts instantiates the engine at the persisted setting, wakes it on the first pointer/key gesture, mirrors the menu audio toggle into the engine, and plays clatter/stings on pin counts, the whir on every reset, the thunk and roll on launch, and clicks on every meter stop and menu button. Files: src/audio.ts, src/main.ts, tests/audio.test.ts. PR #TBD.

Re-pointed the venue from a cold blue-black to a warm machine-room dark: low warm ambient plus a warm directional fill, and a warm SpotLight work-light hung over the pin deck as the hero light so the pins read at the far end. Fog far pushed past the pin deck so it no longer fades to black. The camera was lowered to an eye-level shooting pose looking down-lane (it had been a tiny vanishing-point view). Added an approach floor behind the foul line, a steel ball-return rail, and flat lane-marker inlays (foul line, guide dots, seven chevron aiming arrows). All camera, fov, fog, and pose values live in LANE / SHOT_CAMERA config. The full material detail palette (oiled-wood grain, brass, wear) and the visible pinsetter rig (REQ-040) remain follow-ups.
