String pinsetter

Status: partial

Overview

The string pinsetter is VibePins' signature system and pillar 2. In a real string house, every pin is tied to a thin, high-tensile cord (polyester or Dyneema) attached at the pin's neck, the narrow point above the belly. Each cord runs up through a guide tube to an overhead drive unit roughly two to three meters above the lane. The cord does the resetting: there is no sweep bar and fallen pins never leave the deck. They are simply lifted up and out of the way, then lowered back into place. VibePins reproduces this honestly so the reset reads, unmistakably, as strings rather than a hidden tenpin machine.

The tether

Each of the ten pins is attached at the neck to a cord anchored to a fixed overhead point above its home position. In physics terms this is a distance or rope constraint between the pin's neck and the anchor (Rapier rope/spherical joint or a length-limited distance constraint). Key property: the cord is slack at the throw. Its maximum length is set so that a pin at rest, and a pin knocked over anywhere it would naturally fall on the deck, never feels the cord. The cord only goes taut when a pin is lifted by the reset or dragged far by a tangle.

Free fall on impact

During a throw the cords must not interfere with the pins. Pins fall, slide, and collide as if untethered. This is the slack-cord phase. The string's presence becomes observable only at reset time and in the tangle cases below. If the cords visibly restrain a falling pin during normal play, the slack length is wrong.

Standing vs fallen detection

A real string machine reads pin state from cord tension: a standing pin holds a defined baseline tension, a fallen pin's cord goes slack toward zero. Detection runs in a short window (roughly a third to a half second) after the ball and pins settle, classifying all ten at once.

VibePins mirrors this with an upright test: a pin counts as standing if its up-axis is within a tolerance of vertical AND it is at rest (linear and angular velocity below thresholds) AND it is still on the deck footprint. Otherwise it counts as fallen. The settle window prevents counting a pin that is still wobbling. This standing/fallen result drives both scoring (core loop, pin accounting) and the reset cycle below.

The reset cycle

The reset is a timed sequence, roughly three to five seconds total, with no sweep:

  1. Settle and detect (short window): wait for pins to stop, classify each as standing or fallen.
  2. Recall the rack: reel in the cords and raise the pins up and off the deck. The lift is genuinely cord-tension driven: each pin is raised BY ITS NECK, the point the cord attaches, so a downed pin is dragged up by the neck and HANGS from its cord, swinging and righting itself base-down under gravity as it rises. A pin is never stood upright on the deck first and then elevated. Between balls within a frame, when one or more pins were knocked down, the WHOLE rack is reeled up (the recall-all motion of a real string machine). On a full re-rack (frame end), all ten are lifted too. Once the rack hangs clear aloft, the machine captures the pins it will set and carries them over their home spots.
  3. Reposition: with the deck clear, the drive unit moves the pins it will set down back over their home positions. The knocked-down pins on a between-balls recall do not reposition; they stay reeled up and aloft, cleared out of play.
  4. Lower and ready: lower the held pins onto their home spots in the triangle, release to rest, and hand control back to the player. Between balls only the STANDING pins are lowered (re-spotted on their home spots); the knocked-down pins stay aloft. On a re-rack all ten are lowered home.

The motion is visible and legible: the player sees the cords go taut and the pins rise by the neck (dangling and swinging from the strings), not a curtain wipe and not pins popping upright on the deck. Timing should feel snappy but unmistakably mechanical. The common case (a clean rack) reels straight up and sets with no fuss.

Re-spot the standing pins on their home spots

Between balls in a frame, when one or more pins were knocked down the machine reels up the whole rack, then lowers the STANDING pins back onto their HOME spots and leaves the knocked-down pins reeled up and aloft. A standing pin that a hit nudged off its home spot without toppling it returns to its home spot on the recall (it does not stay at its nudged resting position). The knocked-down pins are cleared out of play via the strings and do not come back down. This matches real string play (confirmed by observing the machine at Pins Mechanical): the rack reels up and the standing pins are re-set on their home spots, the downed pins stay up. It matters for the second and third ball, where the player aims at a specific remaining cluster sitting on its home spots. If no pin was knocked down (a clean miss or pure gutter with the rack untouched), no recall is needed.

Tangles, hangs, and yanks

The strings are not a clean abstraction, and VibePins treats their failure modes as emergent flavor rather than bugs:

Hanging pin
A cord can leave a pin dangling above or beside the deck rather than cleanly fallen or cleanly standing. Pins Mechanical's own house rule is "no untangling of strings," meaning the lane plays it as-is. VibePins resolves a hanging pin by the detection rule in detection: if it is not upright and at rest on the deck, it counts as fallen, even if the string is still holding it aloft.
Neighbor yank
When a falling pin reaches the end of its slack, or when the reset lifts a tangled cluster, a taut cord can tug a neighboring pin. This can occasionally knock down or save a pin that physics alone would not have. Allowed and intended; it is part of the string character. It must arise from the constraint simulation, not from scripted cheating.
Crossed cords
Cords can cross during chaotic falls. Visually this should be tolerated (cords may overlap) without the simulation deadlocking. There is no manual untangle action in v1; the next full re-rack lifts everything and clears the geometry.
Up/down shake recovery on a genuine snag
This is the charm of a real string machine and the observable form of crossed-cord tolerance. It is RARE. In a real string house a snag happens less than once per roughly a thousand reels, and only in one specific way: a downed pin lies across another pin's cord, so when the cord-tension reel-up pulls that second pin up, the two cords snag and the pin cannot rise to its clearance height. A clean rack (the overwhelmingly common case) reels straight up by the necks with NO shake and sets at once. ONLY on a genuine snag does the machine recover: it pays the snagged cords back out a little (the held pins drop), lets GRAVITY swing the snag loose, then reels back up and re-checks, repeating that up/down shake until the rack hangs clear, and only then sets the pins. VibePins reproduces exactly this. After the cord-tension reel-up it reads the live rack for a genuine snag: a pin whose neck failed to rise to within tolerance of its lifted clearance height (its cord is held low by another pin lying across it). If none is snagged the rack sets immediately with no shake. If one is, the machine runs the visible up/down shake (pay the cords out, let the snag swing loose, reel back up) and re-checks. The loop is BOUNDED: after a fixed number of shakes the machine force-clears (sets the rack regardless) so the reset can never hang or deadlock. The recovery controller is pure and deterministic (a settle-hold / lift / verify-lift / shake-down / shake-up state machine with an injected snag verdict and frame counts); the actual snag detection reads from the live constraint sim in the physics adapter (the reeled pin necks), so the recovery is emergent and the shake never fires on a clean rack.

What this system does not do

  • No sweep bar or pin sweep animation. Fallen pins are lifted, never wiped.
  • No free-fall (gravity-only) pinsetter machine. This is a string house specifically.
  • No manual untangle minigame in v1. Tangles resolve at the next re-rack.
  • No simulation of cord wear, motor torque faults, or maintenance. Those are real but out of scope (see out of scope).
